The Objective

This was an academic project during my M.Sc in Data Science, set up as a predictive modelling challenge: given historical match telemetry data from 4 seasons, build a model that could predict baseline match outcomes (Win / Draw / Loss) for a given home team.

The challenge wasn't just accuracy — it was explaining the model's reasoning in a way that made statistical sense to a non-technical audience, which pushed me to focus on interpretable features and explainable methodology rather than just optimising for the headline accuracy number.

"The goal wasn't to build the most complex model. It was to build the most justifiable one — and know exactly why it predicted what it predicted."

Data Exploration

The raw dataset contained 38 variables per match across 4 seasons (~1,520 matches). Initial exploration in SPSS revealed several key characteristics:

  • Strong home advantage signal — home teams won 46% of matches vs. 27% for away teams
  • High multicollinearity between possession percentage and pass completion rate — including both would inflate variance
  • Several variables had 15%+ missing data (injury counts, weather conditions) — these were dropped rather than imputed given the small dataset size
  • Class imbalance: Win (46%), Loss (27%), Draw (27%) — required stratified sampling during model evaluation

Feature Engineering & Selection

1
Correlation Analysis

Ran Pearson and Spearman correlation matrices in SPSS to identify variables most predictive of outcome. Shots on target, goals scored in last 5 matches (rolling form), and defensive actions had the strongest correlations.

2
Feature Construction

Engineered 12 composite features not present in the raw data: 5-match rolling win rate, home/away performance differential, season point trajectory (slope of points per game over last 10 matches), and head-to-head historical advantage index.

3
Stepwise Regression for Selection

Used SPSS stepwise logistic regression to select the final 12-feature subset, removing variables that didn't improve model fit at the 0.05 significance level. This kept the model interpretable and prevented overfitting on the small dataset.

4
Model Evaluation

Evaluated using stratified 5-fold cross-validation to handle class imbalance. Reported accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 per class rather than aggregate accuracy alone — to avoid masking poor Draw prediction performance.

Key Findings

  • Form was more predictive than squad quality — a team's last 5-match win rate outweighed season-average statistics as a predictor of next-match outcome.
  • Draws were the hardest class to predict — F1 of 0.54 for Draw vs. 0.82 for Win and 0.76 for Loss. Draws appear to be more stochastic events than wins or losses.
  • Defensive actions strongly predictive of Away wins — teams that conceded fewer shots on target in away fixtures had a disproportionately higher away win rate, suggesting defensive discipline as the key away-game factor.

Results

  • 78% overall prediction accuracy across Win / Draw / Loss classification
  • Model correctly identified 82% of home wins and 76% of away wins
  • 12 interpretable features — each with clear statistical justification for inclusion
  • Methodology write-up used as a reference example in the university module

What I'd Do Differently

I'd experiment with ensemble methods (XGBoost, Random Forest) alongside the logistic regression to compare accuracy vs. interpretability trade-offs explicitly. I'd also incorporate Elo rating as a baseline feature — it's a well-established signal in sports prediction that I underweighted in favour of raw statistical features.
