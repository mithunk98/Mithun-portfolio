The Problem

The marketing team for an e-commerce client knew their overall conversion rate but had no visibility into where in the buying journey users were dropping off. Google Analytics was in place but the non-technical team couldn't extract the stage-by-stage funnel breakdown they needed to make informed decisions about UI changes and campaign spend.

Every month, the team was making page redesign decisions based on gut feel rather than data — spending budget on optimising pages that weren't actually the problem while the real bottlenecks remained untouched.

"We knew we were losing people somewhere between the product page and checkout. We just didn't know exactly where — or how many."

Data Preparation

The raw data came from the platform's event log — a table of user session events with timestamps, page identifiers, and session IDs. Key preparation steps:

  • Defined the 5-stage funnel: Landing → Product View → Add to Cart → Checkout Start → Purchase
  • Wrote SQL to sessionise events and classify each session by the furthest stage reached
  • Handled multi-visit journeys — users who added to cart in one session and purchased in another counted as a single conversion path
  • Filtered bot traffic using session duration and click velocity thresholds

Dashboard Design

1
Funnel Visualisation

Built a custom funnel chart showing absolute user counts and step-by-step drop-off rates. DAX measures calculated both the absolute drop (users lost at each stage) and the relative drop (percentage of previous-stage users who didn't continue).

2
Segmentation Slicers

Added slicers for device type (mobile/desktop/tablet), traffic source (organic, paid, direct), and product category. This let the marketing team compare funnel performance by channel — revealing that mobile checkout had a 22% higher abandonment rate than desktop.

3
Time Intelligence

DAX time intelligence functions powered week-over-week and month-over-month comparison panels so the team could track whether funnel improvements were actually moving the needle after UI changes shipped.

4
Bottleneck Highlight Cards

Conditional formatting automatically flagged the stage with the worst drop-off in red, making the biggest problem immediately visible without requiring the viewer to interpret the numbers themselves.

Key Findings

  • Checkout start was the biggest drop-off — 68% of users who added to cart never initiated checkout. The form required account creation before purchase, which the data strongly implicated as the barrier.
  • Mobile checkout was broken — 22% worse abandonment on mobile than desktop, traced to an address form that didn't support autofill on iOS.
  • Paid traffic had a higher bounce rate at product pages — suggesting a landing page and ad creative mismatch rather than a product or pricing issue.

Results

  • 3 critical drop-off points identified and ranked by impact
  • Marketing team had a data-backed case to request guest checkout implementation (highest-impact fix)
  • Dashboard became the weekly review artefact for growth and product teams
  • Mobile autofill fix shipped within 2 weeks of the dashboard flagging the issue

What I'd Do Differently

I'd push to instrument the funnel at the component level, not just the page level — knowing that users dropped off on the checkout page is useful, but knowing they dropped off specifically at the payment form would be more actionable. I'd also set up automatic refresh so the dashboard always showed live data rather than requiring a manual export trigger.
